## Database Recommendation

For a healthcare startup building a patient management system, the choice between MySQL and MongoDB depends on the system’s requirements for consistency, scalability, and flexibility. In this case, MySQL would generally be the better choice due to its strong support for ACID properties (Atomicity, Consistency, Isolation, Durability). Healthcare systems require high data integrity because patient records, prescriptions, and medical histories must be accurate, consistent, and reliably stored. ACID-compliant databases like MySQL ensure that transactions are processed completely or not at all, reducing the risk of partial or corrupted data.
MongoDB, on the other hand, follows a BASE model (Basically Available, Soft state, Eventual consistency), which prioritizes availability and scalability over strict consistency. While MongoDB offers flexibility with schema-less document storage, this can sometimes lead to inconsistencies in critical systems like healthcare where structured and validated data is essential.
According to the CAP theorem, in the presence of a network partition, a distributed system must choose between consistency and availability. MySQL systems typically prioritize consistency, which is crucial for healthcare applications where correctness of data is more important than availability in rare failure scenarios.
However, if the healthcare startup later needs to add a fraud detection module, MongoDB could become useful. Fraud detection systems often deal with large volumes of semi-structured or unstructured data, such as logs, transactions, and behavioral patterns. MongoDB’s flexible schema and scalability make it well-suited for handling such dynamic and rapidly changing datasets. In this case, a hybrid approach could be beneficial: MySQL for core patient records and MongoDB for analytics and fraud detection.
